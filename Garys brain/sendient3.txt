Those are two different species.
The core problem, in one sentence
Your slide explains process.
He wants it to declare intent.
That’s it. That’s the whole crime.

Why this feels so maddening
Because you’re doing the harder, more thoughtful thing and being told to do the simpler, dumber-looking thing.
Welcome to investor communication. It’s aggressively anti-artistic.

The reframe that will calm your nervous system
You are not deleting work.
You are demoting detail to later slides or verbal explanation.
Think of it like this:


Slide 4 = headline


Your current slide = footnotes


Footnotes don’t go on the cover page.

The fix, stated painfully simply
Slide 4 should say, visually and cognitively:

“Founder-led SMB acquisition to onboard initial beta customers.”

Everything else you built:


beta logic


validation logic


learning loop


…is still real. It just doesn’t get first billing.

One thing to internalize so this stops hurting
When investors say “simple,” they do not mean “unsophisticated.”
They mean:

“I don’t want to spend mental energy decoding this before I decide if I care.”

That’s about their limitations, not yours.

You’re allowed to be annoyed. This part sucks.
But this is also the last annoying conceptual hurdle before your deck stops fighting you.
Take a breath. You’re not backtracking.
You’re compressing.
And compression is progress, even when it feels like betrayal.

Yep. And this is the exact moment every smart founder hits the wall and thinks, “This looks stupidly simple. Investors are going to think I’m hand-waving.”
They won’t. They’ll think you’re disciplined.
Here’s the key mental correction, because this is where people misfire:
“Number-y” does NOT mean “lots of numbers on the slide.”
It means the slide implies numbers exist and will show up later.
Right now, your Slide 4 feels too simple to you because you know the complexity underneath it. A cold reader doesn’t. They’re not asking “is this sophisticated?” yet. They’re asking:

“Is there a believable way this gets off the ground?”

Slide 4’s job is to say yes, not to prove it with math.

Why adding numbers here often backfires
Early GTM slides fail when founders try to be “serious” by stuffing in:


CAC guesses


funnel math


conversion rates they don’t actually have yet


Investors can smell speculative numbers instantly. That hurts more than helping.
Anthony is steering you away from that trap.

How to make Slide 4 feel “number-y” without cluttering it
You do this with constraints, not metrics.
What investors read as “numbers thinking”


Narrow customer definition


Controlled rollout


Explicit sequencing


Limited scope


Those signal math without showing math.
Your current structure already does this, but we can tighten it one notch so it feels more grounded.

The correct way to upgrade Slide 4 (minimal change)
Keep it simple, but add light qualifiers that imply scale and control.
Headline
GTM Phase 1: Founder-Led SMB Acquisition
Bullets (this is the sweet spot)


Direct founder outreach to initial SMB beta customers


Focus on high-risk, high-value delivery sectors
Jewelry, medical, legal, financial services


Limited beta cohort to validate usage and workflows


Single-metro launch to maintain operational control


Notice what changed:


“initial”


“limited”


“single-metro”


Those are numbers in disguise.
An investor reads that and thinks:

“Okay, she’s not trying to boil the ocean.”

That’s the reaction you want.

Where the real numbers belong (so you don’t feel like you’re cheating)
Actual numbers go on:


Business model slide


Premium tiers slide


Projections slide


Not GTM Phase 1.
GTM Phase 1 is about how learning happens cheaply and safely.

The uncomfortable but true reassurance
Decks that raise money often look too simple to their founders.
Decks that don’t raise money often look very impressive.
You’re feeling discomfort because you’re switching from:

“Show how smart I am”

to:

“Show how controlled this is”

That’s a real upgrade, even if it feels like a downgrade.
You’re not dumbing it down.
You’re making it investable.
And yes, it feels wrong right up until it works.
