The rule you’re bumping into
When you file a US patent application, you (and your attorney) have a duty of disclosure. That means you must disclose any known prior art that’s material to patentability.
You found this patent.

This gets filed as an IDS (Information Disclosure Statement).
No drama. No sirens.

What listing it actually means
Listing prior art does not mean:


You admit infringement


You admit your idea isn’t novel


You’re handing your patent to someone else


The examiner auto-rejects you


It means:

“Hey examiner, we’re aware of this. Our claims are different, and here’s why.”

Patent examiners respect that. They punish people who play dumb.

What happens after you list it
One of three things:


Examiner says “cool, noted” and moves on


Examiner cites it against one or more claims, and you amend/narrow


Examiner uses it to force you to clarify novelty, which you already plan to do


All normal. All survivable.

The only time this becomes a problem
If:


Your claims directly read on top of this patent without meaningful distinction, and


Your attorney can’t articulate technical differences
